Turbo 360 Developer Spotlight
Ryan Byrne

Ryan is a React Native developer and lives in NYC.

What is your current job? What are you working on now, specifically?

I am a freelance fullstack developer. Lately, I have been very focused on react native and mostly working in mobile development.

Why did you decide to become a programmer? How did you initially get introduced to it?

I actually did a CS Minor in College, but in terms of programming, we only did some C++/made some Java Applets and stuff like that. I definitely gained a good foundation of CS core principles and concepts to build.

Did you have any particular role models – someone you looked at and thought, “I want to do what that person is doing”?

DHH. I love his outlook on programming and business. And apparently now he is a race car driver? I mean who’s role model isn’t DHH.

Where did you go to school for your profession? If so, where and what did you major in? If not, where did you learn your skills?

As mentioned I was a CS minor, and additionally I actually took a course over at Velocity to get up to speed with some of the more modern technologies (React/Redux).

What’s been the toughest part of learning to be a developer? Was there ever a point where you were close to throwing in the towel and taking things in a different direction?

I think sometimes newer developers assume that more senior developers somehow know everything in the entire software development industry, and can get overwhelmed by how much there can be to learn. This is clearly not the case, but I think this can frustrate a lot of newer programmers.

What is the most challenging aspect of your job?

I think just how fast the industry changes. It is both a blessing and a curse. You certainly never get bored or run out of new things to learn as a developer, but at the same time it can be a bit tiring (for some, not me because I am a crazy person and love it). If you think about it, the iPhone X just came out the other day to celebrate the 10th anniversary of the iPhone. It is crazy to think about how much the field of app development has advanced and changed over what would be a miniscule period in some other industries.

What are the benefits of being a software developer?

Freedom in two ways. One, is the freedom to work remotely if that is something you so desire. I think there are more opportunities for remote work in programming than in may other fields. For me, it is the freedom to make whatever I want. If I want to make a project or app or site or have some crazy new idea, there is nothing stopping me from doing it (except for time sometimes). Not a lot of other people in other professions can say that.

What advice would you give to someone who is thinking of becoming a programmer?

You need to love doing it. If you are not having fun doing it, maybe it is not the career for you. It’s a bit harsh, but there are so many people who love doing it and will be the ones putting in the time to learn new things and will move ahead.

What do you do for fun?

Program.

What do you like most about developing on Turbo? Which features have been most useful to projects you are working on?

You know, I actually am a ‘fullstack developer'. However, there are a lot of times I just do not want to set up a whole backend and server for a simple fun project. Even some client/professional projects. I can get a project up and running fast and I am not wasting time on parts of the project the client may not really see the returns on. For example, if a small local business wants a site or app they may need a mostly static site/app that looks nice and can capture some form data. Why go overboard when I can put all my time into the front end and deliver the results they can see/feel?
